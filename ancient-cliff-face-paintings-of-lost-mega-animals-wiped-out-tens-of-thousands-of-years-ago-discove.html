Ancient cliff face paintings of lost 'mega animals' wiped out tens of thousands of years ago discove

TENS of thousands of ancient Ice Age paintings discovered in the Amazon rainforest have been revealed ahead of a new documentary.

The paintings cover almost eight miles of a cliff face and are thought to show lots of extinct 'mega animals'.

Such animals include a giant sloth, an early horse, a porcupine, alligators and a prehistoric elephant relative called a mastodon.

It's thought the paintings were drawn by Ice Age people around 12,600 to 11,800 years ago.

They would have been some of the first humans to reach the Amazon.

A red natural pigment called ochre was used to create the stunning art.

The paintings are located in an area called the Serranía La Lindosa.

It's one of the largest rock-art sites in the world and is located above three rock shelters.

The people who created the paintings would have lived at a time when the Amazon was only just transforming into the diverse and hot region we know today.

The mass rock-art discovery was made last year in the rebels-controlled Colombian rainforest.

A team of British and Columbian archaeologists found the rare site and have largely kept it a secret ahead of a documentary coming out this month.

It's called  Jungle Mystery: Lost Kingdoms of the Amazon and can be watched on Channel 4 in the UK.

A survey of the cliff face was said to find tens of thousands of images including that of birds, fish, turtles and lizards.

There's also said to be depictions of people including one wearing a bird mask.

Researchers published an analysis of the cliff face paintings in the Quaternary International journal.

Study co-researcher Mark Robinson, an archaeologist at the University of Exeter, said: "These really are incredible images, produced by the earliest people to live in western Amazonia.

He added: "The paintings give a vivid and exciting glimpse into the lives of these communities.

"It is unbelievable to us today to think they lived among, and hunted, giant herbivores, some which were the size of a small car."

People in the UK can watch a documentary about the paintings on Channel 4 on December 5 at 6:30pm.

Giant sloths – the key facts

Here's what you need to know...

  • Megatherium is the official name for a genus of elephant-sized ground sloths native to South America
  • They're better known as giant growth sloths, and lived from the early Pliocene, an epoch that began 5.333million years ago
  • Sadly, giant sloths became extinct roughly around 8,500 BC
  • There were only a few other land mammals that were bigger than Megatherium, making them one of the world's most interesting megafauna
  • The first fossil specimen of Megatherium was discovered in Argentina in 1788
  • Since then, more fossils have been found across South America, including in Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Guyana, Paraguay, Peru and Uruguay
  • They're one of the biggest land mammals known to have ever existed
  • They could weigh up to 4 tonnes, and measured up to 6 metres (20 foot) in length
  • The creatures were herivorous, and had huge curved claws for pulling down far-away branches
  • Giant sloths largely walked on all fours, but trackways reveal that they were capable of walking on two legs at times too
